[Poor Richard&#39;s Almanack](https:&#x2F;&#x2F;blog.plover.com&#x2F;calendar&#x2F;poor-richards-almanack.html)   

Benjamin Franklin wrote and published[_Poor Richard&#39;s Almanack_](https:&#x2F;&#x2F;en.wikipedia.org&#x2F;wiki&#x2F;Poor%5FRichard%27s%5FAlmanack) annually from 1732 to 1758\. Paper was expensive and printing difficult and time-consuming. The type would be inked, the sheet of paper laid on the press, the apprentices would press the sheet, by turning a big screw. Then the sheet was removed and hung up to dry. Then you can do another printing of the same page. Do this ten thousand times and you have ten thousand prints of a sheet. Do it ten thousand more to print a second sheet. Then print the second side of the first sheet ten thousand times and print the second side of the second sheet ten thousand times. Fold 20,000 sheets into eighths, cut and bind them into 10,000 thirty-two page pamphlets and you have your Almanacks.

As a youth, Franklin was apprenticed to his brother James, also a printer, in Boston. Franklin liked the work, but James drank and beat him, so he ran away to Philadelphia. When James died, Benjamin sent his widowed sister-in-law Ann five hundred copies of the _Almanack_ to sell. When I first heard that I thought it was a mean present but I was being a twenty-first-century fool. The pressing of five hundred almanacks is no small feat of toil. Ann would have been able to sell those _Almanack_s in her print shop for fivepence each, or ₤10 8s. 4d. That was a lot of money in 1735.

In 1748 Franklin increased the size and the price. Here&#39;s a typical page from the 1748 _Almanack_:

[![detailed description in the article](https:&#x2F;&#x2F;proxy-prod.omnivore-image-cache.app&#x2F;0x0,sI01NFW0cKL5o7nh0_Eq4vZ2LZz0kzSyTIlvmmcxaxog&#x2F;https:&#x2F;&#x2F;pic.blog.plover.com&#x2F;calendar&#x2F;poor-richards-almanack&#x2F;jan-1748-th.jpg)](https:&#x2F;&#x2F;pic.blog.plover.com&#x2F;calendar&#x2F;poor-richards-almanack&#x2F;jan-1748.jpg)

Wow, there&#39;s a lot of stuff going on there. Here&#39;s a smaller excerpt, this time from November 1753:

![](https:&#x2F;&#x2F;proxy-prod.omnivore-image-cache.app&#x2F;0x0,s_rLPkjqaU0AIQ1PPGxaOiyHs7fHrF8US9fA12d-AHwM&#x2F;https:&#x2F;&#x2F;pic.blog.plover.com&#x2F;calendar&#x2F;poor-richards-almanack&#x2F;serving-god.png)

The leftmost column is the day of the month, and then the next column is the day of the week, with 2–7 being Monday through Saturday. Sunday is denoted with a letter “G”. I thought this was G for God, but I see that in 1748 Franklin used “C” and in 1752 he used “A”, so I don&#39;t know.

The third column combines a weather forecast and a calendar. The weather forecast is in italic type, over toward the right: “Clouds and threatens cold rains and snow” in the early part of the month. Sounds like November in Philadelphia. The roman type gives important days. For example, November 1 is [All Saints Day](https:&#x2F;&#x2F;en.wikipedia.org&#x2F;wiki&#x2F;All%5FSaints%27%5FDay) and November 5 is the anniversary of the[Gunpowder Plot](https:&#x2F;&#x2F;en.wikipedia.org&#x2F;wiki&#x2F;Gunpowder%5FPlot). November 10 is given as the birthday of King George II, then still the King of Great Britain.

The Sundays are marked with some description in the Christian liturgical calendar. For example, “20 past Trin.” means it&#39;s the start of the 20th week past [Trinity Sunday](https:&#x2F;&#x2F;en.wikipedia.org&#x2F;wiki&#x2F;Trinity%5FSunday).

This column also has notations like “Days dec. 4 32” and “Days dec. 5 h.” that I haven&#39;t been able to figure out. Something about the decreasing length of the day in November maybe? The notation on November 6 says “Day 10 10 long” which is consistent with the sunrise and sunset times Franklin gives for that day. The fourth and fifth columns, labeled “☉ ris” and “☉ set” are the times of sunrise and sunset, 6:55 (AM) and 5:05 (PM) respectively for November 6, ten hours and ten minutes apart as Franklin says.

“☽ pl.” is the position of the moon in the sky. (I guess “pl.” is short for “place”.) The sky is divided into twelve “houses” of 30 degrees each, and when it says that the “☽ pl.” on November 6 is “♓ 25” I think it means the moon is !!\\frac{25}{30}!! of the way along in the house of Pisces on its way to the house of Aries ♈. If you look at the January 1748 page above you can see the moon making its way through the whole sky in 29 days, as it does.

The last column, “Aspects, &amp;c.” contains more astronomy. “♂ rise 6 13” means that Mars will rise at 6:13 that day. (But in the morning or the evening?) ⚹♃♀ on the 12th says that Jupiter is in sextile aspect to Venus, which means that they are in the sky 60 degrees apart. Similarly □☉♃ means that the Sun and Jupiter are in Square aspect, 90 degrees apart in the sky.

Also mixed into that last column, taking up the otherwise empty space, are the famous wise sayings of Poor Richard. Here we see:

&gt; _Serving God is Doing Good to Man,_ 
&gt; _but Praying is thought an easier Service,_ 
&gt; _and therefore more generally chosen_.

Back on the January page you can see one of the more famous ones,_Lost Time is never found again_.

Franklin published an _Almanack_ in 1752, the year that the British[Calendar Act of 1751](https:&#x2F;&#x2F;en.wikipedia.org&#x2F;wiki&#x2F;Calendar%5F%28New%5FStyle%29%5FAct%5F1750) updated the calendar from Julian to Gregorian reckoning. To bring the calendar into line with Gregorian, eleven days were dropped from September that year. I wondered what Franklin&#39;s calendar looked like that month. Here it is with the eleven days clearly missing:

![](https:&#x2F;&#x2F;proxy-prod.omnivore-image-cache.app&#x2F;0x0,sRVB5t3x3bGpDq1kKd5hndb52js-sZ3p5VV56oIcqlec&#x2F;https:&#x2F;&#x2F;pic.blog.plover.com&#x2F;calendar&#x2F;poor-richards-almanack&#x2F;september-1752_720.jpg)

The leftmost day-of-the-month column skips right from September 2 to September 14, as the law required. On this copy someone has added the old numbers in the margin. This page also commemorates the[Great Fire of London](https:&#x2F;&#x2F;en.wikipedia.org&#x2F;wiki&#x2F;Great%5FFire%5Fof%5FLondon), which began September 2, 1666\. Notice that [St. Michael&#39;s Day](https:&#x2F;&#x2F;en.wikipedia.org&#x2F;wiki&#x2F;Michaelmas), which would have been on Friday September 18th in the old calendar, has been moved up to September 29th. In most years _Poor Richard&#39;s Almanack_ featured an essay by Poor Richard, little poems, and other reference material. The 1752_Almanack_ omitted most of this so that Franklin could use the space to instead reprint the entire text of the Calendar Act.

Incidentally Wikipedia tells me that Franklin may have gotten the King&#39;s birthday wrong. Franklin says November 10, but[Wikipedia says November 9](https:&#x2F;&#x2F;en.wikipedia.org&#x2F;wiki&#x2F;George%5FII%5Fof%5FGreat%5FBritain%23cite%5Fnote%2ddates%2d2), and:

&gt; Over the course of George&#39;s life, two calendars were used: the Old Style Julian calendar and the New Style Gregorian calendar. Before 1700, the two calendars were 10 days apart. Hanover switched from the Julian to the Gregorian calendar on 19 February (O.S.) &#x2F; 1 March (N.S.) 1700\. Great Britain switched on 3&#x2F;14 September 1752\. George was born on 30 October Old Style, which was 9 November New Style, but because the calendar shifted forward a further day in 1700, the date is occasionally miscalculated as 10 November.

Ugh, calendars.
